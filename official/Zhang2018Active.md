## Summary
This paper introduces and evaluates four novel adaptive wave-domain active noise control (ANC) algorithms designed for noise cancellation over a spatial region. The proposed methods leverage cylindrical harmonic expansions to represent sound fields and adaptively adjust secondary source signals. Two primary cost functions are explored: minimizing the wave-domain residual signal coefficients and minimizing the acoustic potential energy within the control region. The algorithms are further differentiated by whether they update loudspeaker driving signals directly or update the wave-domain secondary sound field coefficients. Simulation results in both free-field and reverberant environments demonstrate that these wave-domain approaches achieve superior noise reduction and faster convergence compared to conventional multi-point methods, particularly when the number of secondary sources is limited.

## Problem
Traditional active noise control (ANC) systems often focus on minimizing sound pressure at specific points, which leads to inconsistent noise reduction over larger spatial regions. For applications like noise cancellation in vehicles or aircraft, where the control zone is extensive, a method is needed to effectively reduce noise across the entire area. Existing spatial ANC methods, while improving global control, may not offer optimal convergence or performance under all conditions, especially when dealing with complex acoustic environments like reverberant rooms or when the number of available secondary sources is constrained.

## Method
The authors propose four adaptive wave-domain ANC algorithms. The core idea is to represent the sound field within a circular control region using cylindrical harmonics. The primary noise field and the secondary sound field generated by loudspeakers are decomposed into these harmonic coefficients. The residual sound field is then expressed in terms of these coefficients. The algorithms aim to minimize either the sum of the squared residual wave-domain coefficients (ξ₁) or the acoustic potential energy (APE) of the residual sound field (ξₚ) within the control region. For each cost function, two update strategies are developed: updating the loudspeaker driving signals (d) directly, or updating the secondary sound field coefficients (γ) and then deriving the driving signals. This results in four distinct algorithms: Normalized Wave-Domain Direct (NWD-D), Normalized Wave-Domain Mode (NWD-M), Normalized Energy-based Wave-Domain Direct (NEWD-D), and Normalized Energy-based Wave-Domain Mode (NEWD-M). The update equations are derived using steepest descent and normalized least mean squares (LMS) principles. Simulations compare these methods against a conventional normalized multi-point (NMP) algorithm in free-field and reverberant conditions, evaluating noise reduction on the boundary, inside the region, and acoustic potential energy, as well as convergence speed and loudspeaker energy consumption.

## Key results
Simulation results indicate that all four proposed wave-domain ANC algorithms significantly outperform the conventional NMP algorithm in terms of noise reduction over the entire spatial region and convergence speed, both in free-field and reverberant environments. When the number of loudspeakers is sufficient to represent all active modes, the wave-domain algorithms, particularly those updating wave-domain coefficients (NWD-M and NEWD-M), exhibit faster convergence. In scenarios with a limited number of loudspeakers (fewer than the number of active modes), the energy-based wave-domain algorithms (NEWD-D and NEWD-M) demonstrate superior performance in noise attenuation and convergence speed compared to the other methods. Specifically, NEWD-D and NEWD-M consistently achieved better noise reduction, especially when the loudspeaker count was reduced to 7 or 5. The study also noted that wave-domain algorithms can achieve substantial noise reduction across a wide frequency range, though performance can degrade at 'irregular frequencies' where Bessel functions approach zero, a known limitation of single circular array configurations. Loudspeaker energy consumption analysis showed that while all algorithms converge to similar energy levels, energy-based algorithms might consume more energy when resources are limited.

## Strengths
The paper presents a comprehensive theoretical framework for adaptive wave-domain ANC, extending previous work by exploring multiple cost functions and update strategies. The use of wave-domain representation inherently addresses spatial noise control, providing a more global approach than point-based methods. The derived algorithms are shown to be effective in both ideal (free-field) and practical (reverberant) acoustic environments. The investigation into the impact of the number of secondary sources provides valuable insights for real-world implementations where source limitations are common. The comparison with a conventional method highlights the advantages of the proposed wave-domain techniques.

## Limitations
A key limitation identified is the potential for performance degradation at specific 'irregular frequencies' when using a single circular microphone array, due to the properties of Bessel functions. This issue can lead to amplified coefficient errors. The paper acknowledges that this can be mitigated by using alternative array configurations or multiple arrays. Furthermore, the study primarily relies on simulations, and real-world experimental validation, while implied, is not explicitly detailed within the presented text. The analysis of loudspeaker energy consumption, while informative, suggests that energy-based algorithms might require more power in certain limited-source scenarios.

## Relevance to our group
This research is highly relevant to our group's focus on advanced acoustic signal processing, particularly in the areas of active noise control and spatial audio. The wave-domain approach offers a powerful paradigm for achieving broadband noise reduction over extended regions, which is a common challenge in many acoustic applications. Understanding the trade-offs between different cost functions (coefficient minimization vs. energy minimization) and update strategies (direct driving signal vs. mode coefficient updates) provides valuable knowledge for designing future ANC systems. The findings on performance under limited source conditions and in reverberant environments are particularly pertinent for practical system development.

## Notes
The authors use the term 'wave-domain signal processing' to specifically refer to harmonic-based sound field processing, distinguishing it from other wave-field synthesis techniques. They propose four distinct adaptive algorithms by combining two cost functions (minimizing residual coefficients or acoustic potential energy) with two update targets (loudspeaker weights or wave-domain coefficients). The paper highlights that when the number of loudspeakers is sufficient, all methods can achieve similar steady-state noise reduction, but wave-domain coefficient updates offer faster convergence. When loudspeaker count is limited, energy-based wave-domain methods show better performance. Future work is suggested to include stability analysis and extension to 3D using spherical harmonics.
